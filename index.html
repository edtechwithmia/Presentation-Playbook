The Presentation Playbook

Universal principles for creating clear, professional, and compelling presentations — in any program or platform.

🎨

Design & Font

Visual structure and typography set the tone for your entire presentation.

💡

The golden rule: Your slides are a visual aid, not a document. Every design decision should support your message — not distract from it.

See the Difference

🌟💫
The Benefits of Exercise!!!
exercise is REALLY good 4 u!!!! it helps your heart and brain and muscles and lots more. scientists say you should do it every day because it makes you feel better and live longer and lose weight and be happy. also it helps with stress too. and sleep. don't forget sleep!!!
Exercise Improves Long-Term Health
• Reduces cardiovascular disease risk by up to 35%
• Improves mood, sleep quality, and cognitive function
• Recommended: 150 min of moderate activity per week
What's wrong here: Decorative emoji, a cursive font, informal lowercase text, exclamation marks, and a glaring yellow background all undermine credibility. The content is buried in unformatted prose with no visual hierarchy.
Fonts

Choose Readable Fonts

Stick to clean, professional typefaces. Sans-serif fonts (Calibri, Helvetica, Lato) work well for body text and slide content.

Hierarchy

Create Visual Hierarchy

Use larger, bolder text for headings and smaller, lighter text for supporting points. This guides the audience's eye naturally.

Consistency

Limit Your Font Choices

Use 1–2 fonts maximum throughout. Mixing many fonts looks chaotic and unprofessional.

Size

Minimum Font Sizes

  • Slide titles: 28–36pt
  • Body text: 20–24pt
  • Captions/labels: 16–18pt

If it's hard to read on your screen, it'll be impossible from the back of the room.

Alignment

Align Elements Consistently

Use your software's alignment tools to line up text and images neatly. Misaligned elements make a slide look rushed.

White Space

Embrace Empty Space

Don't fill every corner. White space reduces visual clutter and helps your audience focus on what matters.

Font Personality: A Quick Guide

Sans-serifClean, modern, easy to read on screens✅ Best for slides
SerifFormal, classic — use sparingly for titles⚠️ Titles only
DecorativeFun but hard to read at small sizes❌ Avoid
All CapsUSE SPARINGLY FOR SHORT LABELS ONLY⚠️ Use sparingly

Choosing Color

Color communicates before a single word is read. Use it intentionally.

🎨

A simple rule: Choose a background, a primary text color, and one accent color. Repeat those throughout. That's a color scheme.

See the Difference

Climate Change Impacts
Rising temperatures are causing widespread disruption to ecosystems worldwide.
Sea levels have risen by 20cm since 1900 and continue to accelerate.
Extreme weather events are now 5× more frequent than in the 1980s.
Climate Change Impacts
Widespread disruption to global ecosystems
Sea levels +20cm since 1900 and accelerating
Extreme weather events 5× more frequent than 1980s
What's wrong here: The rainbow gradient background fights with every text color. Yellow text on a multi-color background has near-zero contrast and is essentially unreadable, especially when projected on a screen.

Contrast is Non-Negotiable

Text must be easy to read against its background. Low contrast is the #1 readability mistake in student presentations.

✅ Dark background,
light text — readable
✅ Light background,
dark text — readable
❌ Similar tones —
very hard to read
❌ Yellow + white —
invisible!

✅ Do

  • Use 2–3 colors maximum
  • Keep background consistent across slides
  • Use your accent color to highlight key ideas
  • Check contrast before presenting

❌ Don't

  • Use more than 4–5 colors total
  • Put text over busy, patterned backgrounds
  • Use red and green together (colorblind-unfriendly)
  • Use neon or very bright colors for large text areas

Amount of Text

Less is almost always more. Your slides support your message — they don't replace it.

🗣

Remember: If your audience is reading your slides, they're not listening to you. Keep text minimal so their attention stays on what you're saying.

See the Difference

The Importance of Sleep for Academic Performance
Sleep is one of the most important factors affecting a student's academic performance. Research has consistently shown that students who get 8 or more hours of sleep per night perform significantly better on tests, assignments, and in class participation than students who sleep fewer than 6 hours. Sleep deprivation leads to decreased concentration, impaired memory consolidation, reduced problem-solving ability, and increased emotional reactivity. Despite this evidence, surveys indicate that up to 70% of college students are chronically sleep-deprived. Students often sacrifice sleep in favor of studying or screen time, not realizing that this tradeoff actually undermines their goals. Establishing consistent sleep schedules and avoiding screens before bed can have a measurable positive impact on GPA.
Sleep & Academic Performance
• 8+ hrs → significantly better test scores & memory
• 70% of college students are chronically sleep-deprived
• Sleep loss impairs concentration, recall, & mood
(Walker, 2017; American Sleep Association, 2023)
What's wrong here: This is an essay paragraph pasted directly onto a slide. The audience will either read it (and stop listening) or feel overwhelmed and tune out entirely. Dense text suggests the presenter doesn't know their material well enough to summarize it.

The 6×6 Guideline

A widely-used rule of thumb: aim for no more than 6 bullet points per slide and no more than 6 words per bullet. Think of bullets as headlines, not sentences.

Headlines

Use Short, Active Titles

Slide titles should state the main point, not just the topic. "Sleep Deprivation Harms Memory" beats "The Effects of Sleep."

Bullets

Bullets Are Keywords

Use bullet points as memory cues for yourself and visual anchors for your audience — not as full paragraphs to be read aloud.

One Idea

One Idea Per Slide

Each slide should communicate a single main point. If you find yourself writing "and also..." you probably need a new slide.

Using Figures & Images

Visuals can make complex information immediately clear — if used well.

📸

Quality over quantity. One strong, relevant image is far more effective than several mediocre or unrelated ones.

See the Difference

Deforestation Rates in the Amazon
Deforestation has been increasing rapidly since the 1970s. Large areas of forest are cleared every year for agriculture and logging purposes which is very bad for biodiversity and contributes to climate change globally speaking.
Amazon Deforestation
• 17% of Amazon lost in 50 years
• Primary driver: agricultural expansion
• Accelerates global carbon emissions
Figure 1. Aerial view of deforested land in the Amazon, Brazil. From Ibama (2016). CC BY 2.0 via Wikimedia Commons.
What's wrong here: The image is stretched (distorted aspect ratio), pixelated, and has no caption or credit. The text beside it is prose rather than bullet points. Together, these make the slide hard to parse and look unprofessional.

When Does a Figure Need a Credit?

A simple rule: if you didn't create it, credit it.

  • Charts or graphs you made from your own data: No credit needed, but label axes and include units.
  • Images, diagrams, or figures from another source: Always include a credit — at minimum: source name and year.
  • Stock photos (royalty-free): Credit the platform or photographer as good academic practice.
  • Charts or data from a published source: Credit as you would any reference (see APA Sources section).

APA 7 Figure Caption Format

Place a caption directly below or beside the image in smaller text (14–16pt). Format:

Figure 1. Brain activity during learning tasks. Adapted from Smith & Jones (2020, p. 45). Copyright 2020 by Academic Press.
Figure 2. Annual deforestation rates, 2000–2023. From Brazil's National Institute for Space Research (INPE, 2023).

✅ Do

  • Use high-resolution, undistorted images
  • Make figures large enough to see from a distance
  • Label all graph axes and include units
  • Credit all external images and figures

❌ Don't

  • Stretch or distort images (hold Shift when resizing)
  • Use pixelated or low-quality images
  • Decorate with unrelated clip art or stock photos
  • Forget to credit figures from external sources

Citing Sources: APA 7th Edition

Academic integrity applies to presentations just as it does to papers.

⚠️

Why it matters: Citing sources isn't just required — it builds credibility. It shows your audience you've done your research and allows them to verify findings. APA 7th edition (2020) is the current standard in many health, social science, and education fields.

See the Difference

Mental Health on Campus
1 in 5 college students experiences anxiety or depression
Only 40% of students who need help seek counseling services
Peer support programs reduce dropout rates by 30%
[ no sources cited — where did these numbers come from? ]
Mental Health on Campus
1 in 5 college students experiences anxiety or depression (NAMI, 2023)
Only 40% who need help seek counseling services (Lipson et al., 2022)
Peer support programs reduce dropout rates by 30% (Chen & Park, 2021)
What's wrong here: Every statistic is a factual claim about the world — but there's no indication where these numbers come from. Without citations, the audience can't verify the data, and the presenter's credibility suffers significantly.

APA 7th: In-Text Citations on Slides

Every time you reference a specific fact, statistic, or idea from a source, add a brief in-text citation on that slide. The format depends on how many authors the work has:

1 author(Last, Year)
(Walker, 2017)
2 authors(Last & Last, Year)
(Smith & Jones, 2020)
3 or more authors(First Author et al., Year)
(Lipson et al., 2022)
Organization / group author(Organization, Year)
First use: (National Alliance on Mental Illness [NAMI], 2023)
After that: (NAMI, 2023)
Direct quote(Last, Year, p. #)
(Brown, 2019, p. 112)
No author listed("Short Title," Year)
("Climate Trends," 2022)
No date available(Last, n.d.)
(National Archives, n.d.)

Your References Slide

Your final slide must be titled References and list every source cited. APA 7th uses a hanging indent (first line flush left, subsequent lines indented ~0.5"). Font can be smaller (14–16pt) to fit more entries. Common formats:

Source TypeReference Format & Example
Journal Article
Author, A. A., & Author, B. B. (Year). Title of article. Journal Name, Volume(Issue), pp–pp. https://doi.org/xxxxx
Lipson, S. K., Lattie, E. G., & Eisenberg, D. (2022). Increased rates of mental health service utilization. Psychiatric Services, 70(1), 60–63. https://doi.org/10.1176/appi.ps.201800077
Book
Author, A. A. (Year). Title of book: Subtitle (Edition, if not 1st). Publisher.
Walker, M. (2017). Why we sleep: Unlocking the power of sleep and dreams. Scribner.
Chapter in an Edited Book
Author, A. A. (Year). Title of chapter. In E. E. Editor (Ed.), Title of book (pp. #–#). Publisher.
Brown, T. (2019). Sleep and cognition. In J. L. Smith (Ed.), Handbook of sleep research (pp. 105–120). Academic Press.
Website / Webpage
Author, A. A., or Organization. (Year, Month Day). Title of page. Site Name. URL
National Alliance on Mental Illness. (2023, September 5). Mental health by the numbers. NAMI. https://www.nami.org/mhstats
Government or Org Report
Agency. (Year). Title of report. Publisher. URL
Centers for Disease Control and Prevention. (2023). Physical activity facts. U.S. Department of Health & Human Services. https://www.cdc.gov/physicalactivity/data/facts.htm
Figure / Image from a Source
Slide caption: Figure #. Description. From Author (Year), Title, p. #. Copyright Year by Publisher. [or: Adapted from / In the public domain.]
Figure 1. Amazon deforestation rates, 2000–2020. From Brazil's National Institute for Space Research (INPE, 2022). In the public domain.

Key APA 7th Changes to Know

  • 3+ authors → et al. immediately: Use "et al." from the very first in-text citation (APA 6th used the full author list on the first mention).
  • DOIs as hyperlinks: Always include a DOI when available, formatted as a full hyperlink: https://doi.org/…
  • No "Retrieved from": Drop this phrase before URLs — just include the URL directly.
  • No publisher location for books: City and state/country are no longer included — just the publisher name.
  • Up to 20 authors: List all authors up to 20 in the References entry. For 21+, list the first 19, then "…" then the last author.

Where Do Sources Belong?

  • Specific statistics or data → In-text citation on the slide where the stat appears
  • Images, charts, figures from external sources → APA figure caption directly below the image
  • General claims backed by research → In-text citation; full entry on References slide
  • All sources together → Final References slide with hanging indent, APA 7 format
📌

Not sure if something needs a citation? Ask: "Did I know this before I started researching?" If the answer is no — cite it. When in doubt, over-cite rather than under-cite.

Narration & Delivery

You are the presentation. Your slides are just the backdrop.

🚫

The #1 rule: Do not read your slides. Your audience can read. They came to hear your insight, explanation, and perspective — not a recitation of what's already on screen.

Slides vs. What You Say

Think of your slides as chapter titles — they orient the audience. Your words are the story. What you say should expand on, not repeat, what's on screen.

On the slide

"Exercise reduces heart disease risk"

What you say

"The AHA found that just 30 minutes of moderate exercise five days a week reduces heart disease risk by up to 35%. That's achievable for most people — and one of the most cost-effective health interventions we know of."

Preparation

Know Your Material

Practice enough that you don't need to read from notes or slides. Familiarity builds confidence, and confidence keeps your audience engaged.

Eye Contact

Look at Your Audience

Glance at your slides to stay oriented, but speak to the people in the room — not to the screen. Eye contact builds connection and shows confidence.

Pace

Slow Down

Nerves make most people speak too fast. Pause intentionally between key points. Silence is not awkward — it gives your audience time to absorb what you said.

Recorded

For Recorded Narration

Script your key points but don't sound scripted. Record practice runs and listen back. Aim for a conversational tone — as if explaining to a friend, not reading a paper.

Speaker Notes

Use Speaker Notes

Most presentation software has a notes panel. Write talking points there — not the full script. These help you stay on track without reading word-for-word.

Transitions

Signal Your Transitions

Tell your audience when you're moving to a new point: "Now let's look at..." or "Building on that..." This keeps them oriented and engaged.
